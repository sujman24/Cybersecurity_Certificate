Course Topics

Cybersecurity is something of utmost importance in this modern, digitised world. It basically aims at protection against cyber-attacks, focusing on systems, networks, and data. Since practically every industry in modern times has shifted its axis of operation toward digital infrastructure, cybersecurity has also grown to a full ability to curb new, sophisticated threats. In the absence of cybersecurity, it would have disastrous effects on businesses, individuals, and even nations. For example, in 2021 alone, ransomware attacks have cost the world some $20 billion in damages, with affected fields ranging from healthcare to energy.

The most significant incident recently was the SolarWinds attack in 2020, where hackers of high order and of a sophisticated nature breached major US government and corporate networks by way of a compromised software update. That has shown how important cybersecurity is in terms of data sensitive to national security or corporate-sensitive data. Various governments have now developed national strategies for cybersecurity to prevent incidents of this type.

New career paths and associated industries have also been born, such as cybersecurity analyst, ethical hacker, and threat intelligence expert. With each passing year, as cyber attacks increase in frequency and sophistication, the demand for skilled manpower and quality security technologies has risen equally, positioning cybersecurity as one of the fastest-moving and most critical sectors of the modern economy.

Understanding cybersecurity fundamentals provides the foundation for building a secure network. These basics include concepts like the CIA Triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability), which are critical principles in cybersecurity. Confidentiality ensures that data is only accessible to authorised users, integrity maintains data accuracy and trustworthiness, and availability ensures reliable access to data when needed.

Additionally, the principle of least privilege (POLP) is essential. POLP dictates that users should only have access to the information and resources necessary for their role. This is a preventative measure to reduce the potential damage if a user's account is compromised. For instance, in a 2014 breach, hackers used a third-party vendor's credentials to access Target's network, highlighting the importance of restricting access to sensitive information.

Cybersecurity fundamentals also cover encryption, multi-factor authentication, and intrusion detection systems (IDS), which are crucial tools for securing data and detecting potential threats early. Knowledge of these tools can prevent breaches, secure sensitive information, and help organisations quickly respond to incidents.

Among different ways of securing online accounts, perhaps Two-Factor Authentication ranks first. It involves users proving their identity through two different steps: something that users know, like a password, and something they have, such as a phone or hardware token. In the case of 2FA, even if the password gets compromised, then to an attacker will require the second factor to gain access.

Several big companies are implementing 2FA. For example, following a wave of high profile Twitter hacks in 2020, Twitter mandated all its employees with sensitive tools to enable 2FA. Google offers this feature via its authenticator app, which has provided a much needed security boost to Gmail account users. An additional layer of verification has helped greatly in reducing the complaints pertaining to unauthorised access and cases of identity theft.

The use of fingerprint or facial recognition, which has become very common, added both convenience and security to biometric 2FA. Several tech giants took the step of integrating biometric 2FA methods into their gadgets and services, notably Apple and Microsoft. As no security measure is perfect, 2FA introduces a big obstacle for attackers, hence being one of the most advisable measures in modern cybersecurity.

Network security is a critical concern and strategy used to prevent unauthorised access to any network resources. In general, the usability, reliability, and integrity of network infrastructure can be protected by strategies and tools. Firewalls, intrusion detection systems, and virtual private networks are some of the key components of network security.

The significance of network security can be combined using several cases. It was the worm Stuxnet that attacked Iranian nuclear facilities. A very critical vulnerability in the network was exposed by this attack, really proving the significance of having network endpoints secured. Network segmentation is another efficient approach in which the division of a network is performed into smaller segments.

Today, it is gaining interest in zero-trust architecture. Zero trust assumes that no user or device should, by default, be trusted even if they are inside the network perimeter. This model is designed for constant verification of users and devices to make it hard for attackers to move laterally inside a network.

Data encryption is a way in which information is secured through conversion to code. The code is intended to be decoded by those authorised to have access and through the correct key. Encryption has become very important in protecting sensitive information from such industries as finance, health, and government.

The Snowden revelations in 2013 are among the largest examples of how essential encryption is. This was somewhat a shock, at least insofar as it came out that the US government had an access point to private data. Generally speaking, this became a wake-up call to make data encryption a priority for organisations around the world in order to protect sensitive information from getting out. Nowadays, data encryption either in rest or in transit that is, while stored or transferred through the networks widely performed to prevent unauthorised disclosure of data.

Encryption standards like AES-256 are highly resistant to brute force attack strategies. The encryption of WhatsApp and Signal messages ensures users' privacy in communication. In all such cases, even data intercepts cannot make it readable for unauthorised users, hence making encryption the most powerful tool in cybersecurity.

Different encryption techniques address various security needs. Symmetric encryption, which includes AES, involves the use of the same key in encryption and decryption; thus, it can handle large chunks of data with efficiency. Asymmetric encryption employs a key pair, like RSA, wherein a public key is used in encryption, and a private key for decryption; hence, one can securely exchange data.

Another encryption method involves Elliptic Curve Cryptography, which enables strong security with smaller keys, hence efficient for mobile devices. In fact, there are many applications of ECC where the computing power is at a premium. It enables a high level of security without large processing power.

Encryption is among the most critical building blocks in the attempt to protect sensitive information. For example, SSL/TLS encryption enables users to securely connect via the Internet. It encrypts data sent between the user's browser and the server, as reflected with the HTTPS protocol. The practice of encryption securely by any organisation handling sensitive or regulated information.

NotPetya ransomware is a famous example that caused billions of dollars in damage to companies like Maersk and FedEx. Threat detection systems help in finding these threats as early as possible to help organisations reduce the impact. AI-powered threat detection systems detect abnormal network activities, making it proactive for cybersecurity.

Real-time threat detection is mandatory in the current landscape. Due to this, most organisations make use of SOCs that monitor the threats round the clock to always ensure timely detection and neutralisation of threats to protect their assets and data.

Firewalls are the basic building blocks of network security. They serve as enforcers, observing and controlling all incoming and outgoing network traffic according to a set of predetermined security rules. Firewalls block malicious traffic and deny unauthorised users access to private networks.

Modern firewalls allow deep packet inspection, intrusion prevention, which blocks malicious traffic in real-time, and other such features and capabilities. Firewalls may inspect just the header information within packets or examine the data inside each packet. They can detect and block potential threats before they get into the network and thus are very important for organisations that have sensitive information to be secured.

As working from home began to gain momentum, firewalls followed suit, providing support for security remote access and allowing workers out of the office to be protected from outside threats. Strong firewall protection remains one of the key elements of network integrity.
